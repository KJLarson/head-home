Learn About Homelessness

What Are Some Causes of Homelessness?

  • Lack of affordable housing
  • Low income
  • Health problems
  • Domestic abuse
  • Discrimination
  • Decreased amount of public assistance
  • Lack of employment opportunities
  • Natural disaster

Types of Homelessness

  • Chronic Homelessness
    • People most like the stereotyped "skid-row" homeless
    • Likely to use shelters more like long-term housing than for emergencies
    • Likely to be older
    • Often unemployed
    • Often have disabilities and substance abuse problems
    • Small portion of the homeless population
  • Transitional Homelessness
    • People who use a shelter only once, for a short period of time
    • Likely to be younger
    • Often part of the precariously housed population ("couch surfers" who stay with friends and family)
    • Become homeless because of a catastrophic event
    • Makes up the largest part of the homeless population
  • Episodic Homelessness
    • People who are often in and out of homelessness
    • Often young
    • Often chronically unemployed
    • Frequently have medical, mental health, and substance abuse problems

Myths and Facts About Homelessness

There are many myths, or false ideas, about homelessness. Many of them are damaging to people who are experiencing homelessness and society in general.

These are some of the common myths. Click on each one to see the true answer. Click on the myth again to hide the truth.

Truth:

Nobody has a goal of being homeless. People are homeless for a wide variety of reasons, many of which are at least partly and often largely beyond a personâ€™s control. Yes, poor choices can contribute to homelessness. But outside circumstances strongly influence those choices.

Truth:

Once a person or family loses their home, it is very difficult to get back into housing. Most people lose their housing because of financial issues.Getting a job without an address, phone number, shower, or clean clothes is very challenging. Other things that can add to difficulties getting out of homelessness include health problems, legal issues, and lack of transportation. People who are homeless don't want to stay homeless, though some might give up trying to find housing after they aren't able to access services after months or years of trying.

Truth:

Many homeless people report having substance abuse issues, but most say the problem started after losing their housing and was not the cause of their homelessness. It is common for people to begin using drugs and alcohol in order to escape the difficulties of being homeless. Some people are able to stop their addiction when they find stable housing, as they no longer need it to cope.

Truth:

Getting a job is very difficult for a homeless person, as many do not have access to a shower, clean clothes, transportation, or a permanent addess or phone number. Some people may have additional barriers, like a criminal past, disabilities, or a lack of education. Sometimes, even when a person does have income from a job, disability, and/or VA benefits it is not enough to afford housing.

Truth:

Living on the street is a lot of work. It can take all day to find food or travel to another part of the city. A homeless person must also protect their belongings while they do anything because they usually do not have a secure place to lock stuff up. All this is often done while being sleep deprived, cold, and/or hungry.

Truth:

It only takes one event to put someone in a situation where they can no longer afford housing. Many homeless people have had solid jobs and families. Even people who are financially secure can lose their home after one or more unfortunate events.
